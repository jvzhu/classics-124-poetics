# Aristotle — catharsis and the defense of mimesis

**Primary text:** *Poetics*, esp. chs. 4, 6, 9, 13–14.

## The argument

Aristotle's *Poetics* is best read as a point-by-point answer to Plato's *Republic* X, though it never names Plato directly.

1. **Mimesis is natural and cognitive, not merely appetitive.** Ch. 4: humans take pleasure in imitation because imitation is a mode of *learning* — recognizing "this is that." Representation is reframed from ontological deficiency (Plato) to epistemic activity.
2. **Catharsis (ch. 6).** Tragedy effects a "purgation" (or "clarification" — the term is contested) of pity and fear through the representation of pitiable and fearful events. Where Plato worried that mimetic identification weakens the soul's rational governance, Aristotle claims the opposite: structured emotional arousal, properly shaped by plot (*mythos*), has a stabilizing effect.
3. **Universals over particulars (ch. 9).** Poetry is "more philosophical" than history because it speaks to what a certain kind of person would probably or necessarily do — it deals in universals, not the mere fact of what happened.
4. **The best plots involve harm within philia (ch. 14)** — the tragic action is most effective when it occurs between people bound by love or kinship, since this generates pity and fear most directly.

## The external frame

Aristotle's defense of tragedy's darkest content (murder, incest, self-blinding) is licensed by an appeal to *structure* — plot, recognition, reversal — as the container that converts raw transgressive material into a beneficial experience. The moral safety of the content is not intrinsic to the content; it is manufactured by the formal frame (unified plot, proper magnitude, probability/necessity) that Aristotle insists surrounds it. This is structurally identical to Zhang Zhupo's insistence that the "obscene" material in *Jin Ping Mei* is safe, even instructive, *because of* the moral architecture (bitter irony, retributive plotting, structural parallelism) he claims to find organizing the novel.

## Note on catharsis translation debate

Whether *katharsis* means medical purgation, religious purification, or intellectual clarification changes how strong a claim this is. Flag this ambiguity rather than picking a side prematurely — it matters for how directly Aristotle's account maps onto Zhang Zhupo's claim that reading *Jin Ping Mei* rightly produces moral clarity ("懲戒/warning") rather than titillation.
