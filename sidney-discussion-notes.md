# Discussion Notes: Sidney's *Defence of Poesy*

*Reading for April 5 / 7 — Vivien Zhu*

## I. Sidney's core move (pp. 1–48)

The argument rests on one inversion: poetry doesn't answer to nature, it exceeds it. The poet is a "maker" producing a "golden world" against nature's "brazen" one.

Worth raising in discussion: **this is an anti-mimetic theory dressed in classical vocabulary.** Sidney sounds like he's defending imitation (the standard Renaissance framework), but his actual claim — that poetry's value lies in exceeding rather than copying reality — quietly abandons mimesis as the ground of value.

*Opening question:* does anyone else read the "golden world" passage as doing something more radical than Sidney's own rhetoric admits?

The triangulation with philosophy and history (poetry teaches *and* delights, uniquely) is the more conventional part of the argument — useful mainly as a foil for what he does next.

## II. The refutation and its limits (pp. 49–end)

Sidney's answer to the "poetry as nurse of abuse" charge: fault lies with bad poets/readers, not poetry itself.

**Discussion point:** this move relocates responsibility without building any mechanism for producing a better reader. Sidney assumes virtuous reading follows naturally from well-made poetry — this is the load-bearing weakness if you want to press the text. He never asks what happens when the poem's content and a reader's disposition don't align.

## III. Comparative pressure point (if the seminar goes there)

If discussion opens onto other traditions defending transgressive fiction, the *Jin Ping Mei* commentarial tradition is a sharp contrast case — not because it's exotic, but because it exposes exactly the gap in Sidney's argument:

- **Zhang Zhupo (1695 commentary):** defends the novel's explicit content via a Xunzian logic — human nature is evil, so unflinching depiction serves moral diagnosis rather than corruption. Structurally the mirror image of Sidney: same defensive move (transgressive content redeemed by a stable moral frame), opposite content-claim (accurate depiction of vice, not an improved golden world).

- Critically, Zhang doesn't just argue this — he built a **reading method** (line-by-line explication, structural commentary) to try to produce the virtuous reader Sidney only assumes into existence. Arguably a more honest response to the "nurse of abuse" problem than Sidney gives.

- **Naifei Ding's critique** (*Obscene Things*, 2002) argues this whole defensive apparatus is not neutral — Zhang's moral frame actively manufactures Pan Jinlian as an abstract "lascivious woman" (*yinfu*), erasing her actual status as a bondmaid-concubine in the late-Ming labor market. The moralizing defense doesn't just protect the text; it produces the gender ideology it claims only to explain.

> **Throughline:** defenses of poetry/fiction that redeem transgressive content by appeal to a stable didactic frame — whether Sidney's improving golden world or Zhang's diagnostic realism — tend to naturalize whatever gender and moral order that frame assumes. The interesting critical work happens at the seam where that frame gets forced open (Ding on Zhang; possibly worth asking where, if anywhere, Sidney's text has an equivalent seam).

## Questions to bring to seminar

1. Does Sidney's "golden world" survive as a coherent theory once you note it isn't really about mimesis at all?
2. Is the Plato refutation (pp. 49ff.) actually successful, or does it just relocate the problem onto readers Sidney never characterizes?
3. Where does the unities critique of English drama fit argumentatively — is it consistent with the golden-world theory, or does it smuggle back in a demand for realism Sidney elsewhere rejects?
