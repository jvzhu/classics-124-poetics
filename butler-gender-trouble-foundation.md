# Judith Butler, *Gender Trouble* (1990) — theoretical foundation

## Core claims used in this project

1. **Performativity, not expression.** Gender is not the expression of a prior, interior identity — it is constituted by the repeated stylization of acts, gestures, and enactments over time. There is no "doer behind the deed"; the doer is constructed *through* the deed's repetition.
2. **The regulatory frame precedes the "natural" category it claims to describe.** Butler's central move against a naturalized sex/gender binary is to show that the categories treated as prior, biological givens are in fact produced and stabilized by the very discourses (medical, legal, cultural) that claim only to be describing them.
3. **Subversive repetition.** Because gender is constituted through repetition, it is also open to subversive repetition — parody, drag, and other performances that repeat the norm with a difference can expose the norm's own constructedness rather than simply reinforcing it.

## Why this is the connective tissue for the project

The pattern Butler identifies — an external frame that produces the very category (gender, "the natural") it presents itself as merely describing — is structurally identical to the pattern this project traces across Western poetics and the *Jin Ping Mei* commentarial tradition: a frame (civic pedagogy, plot structure, authorial genius, decorum, Zhang Zhupo's structural theory) that produces the moral category (harmful/edifying, obscene/instructive) it claims only to be reading off the text. Butler supplies the sharpest available theoretical vocabulary for naming this pattern precisely, because her account was built specifically to resist the temptation to treat "produced" categories as if they were prior and natural.

## Specific application: cross-dressing in *Jin Ping Mei*

Butler's performativity thesis is directly testable against scenes of cross-dressing and gender transgression in the novel — moments where gender is visibly *staged* rather than simply inhabited are exactly the kind of pressure point Butler's framework is built to read. As noted in the Naifei Ding file, a sustained Butlerian reading of cross-dressing specifically in *Jin Ping Mei* does not appear to be a fully claimed feature of the existing Anglophone secondary literature — this is the project's proposed original contribution, connecting Ding's discourse-analytic account of obscenity to a performativity-specific reading of gender enactment.

## Genealogical note

This connection is not purely a matter of theoretical convenience: it traces back to direct training in performativity theory (undergraduate honors thesis advised by Judith Butler), which is part of why this framework, rather than a more generic gender-theory citation, is the operative lens for the project.

## Caution

Butler's framework was developed for a specific late-20th-century Western context (medical/legal discourse on sex, drag performance as a site of subversion). Applying it to a 17th-century Chinese novel and its commentarial tradition requires care not to flatten historical specificity — the claim here is a structural analogy (both are cases of frame-produces-category), not an assertion that Ming-Qing China shared Butler's exact discursive apparatus. Any write-up should state this caveat explicitly rather than let the comparison do more work than it can bear.
