# Beauty, Eros, and the Ascent: *Phaedrus*, *Symposium*, Plotinus

*Covers: Plato,* Phaedrus *249d–257b •* Symposium *210a–212c • Plotinus,* Enneads *1.6*
*Syllabus week: Mar. 15, 17 — "the concept of beauty"*

## I. *Phaedrus* 249d–257b — the soul's wings and the sight of beauty

This picks up from the earlier *Phaedrus* reading (274b–278e, on writing) but jumps back to the dialogue's central myth. The soul, before embodiment, traveled with the gods and glimpsed the Forms directly, including Beauty itself. Embodied souls have mostly forgotten this — except that **Beauty alone among the Forms has a visible earthly counterpart**: when a lover sees a beautiful body or face, something in the soul is stirred to recollection (*anamnesis*) of the Form it once beheld. This is why beauty, uniquely among the Forms, provokes an overwhelming, almost violent bodily response — Socrates describes the soul's wings beginning to sprout again, an itching, feverish sensation.

Two things worth flagging for comps:

- **Beauty is epistemically privileged.** Justice, wisdom, and the other Forms "have no light" in their earthly images — you can't fall in love with an instance of justice the way you fall in love with a beautiful person. Beauty is the one Form that shines through into sensible experience with enough clarity to trigger recollection. This gives eros a unique cognitive function: erotic desire is, structurally, the beginning of philosophy.
- **The account is explicitly embodied and physiological**, not abstract — Socrates describes real bodily symptoms (heat, itching, the pain of desire) as part of the soul's re-growth process. This complicates any reading of Platonic eros as merely "spiritual" in a way that dismisses the body — the ascent runs *through* intense bodily response, not around it.

## II. *Symposium* 210a–212c — Diotima's ladder

This is the culmination of Diotima's speech (reported by Socrates), often called the "Ladder of Love." The ascent runs: love of one beautiful body → recognition that all beautiful bodies share the same beauty → love of beautiful souls (valued above bodies) → love of beautiful practices, laws, institutions → love of the sciences/forms of knowledge → finally, direct vision of **Beauty itself** — eternal, unchanging, not beautiful in one respect and ugly in another, not beautiful relative to some things and not others.

**Key structural point:** every rung of the ladder is a *generalization* from the one before — you start from a particular body and progressively abstract away from particularity until you reach the Form. This makes the ladder a theory not just of beauty but of **how erotic attachment to a particular person is, properly conducted, a training in abstraction** — which is a strange and often uncomfortable claim, since it implies the "highest" love has moved entirely away from the person who started the process.

**Discussion angle worth raising:** the ladder explicitly instrumentalizes the individual beloved — they're a rung to be climbed past, not an endpoint. This is worth sitting with for your own work: Diotima's model treats particular embodied desire as valuable *only* insofar as it's a stepping-stone to something that erases the particularity (and the body) that made it desire in the first place. That's a very different structure from *Phaedrus*, where the beloved's actual face and body remain present throughout — the *Phaedrus* lover never stops looking at the specific person, even as recollection expands outward.

## III. Plotinus, *Enneads* 1.6, "On Beauty" — beauty without proportion

Plotinus (3rd century CE) is working roughly six centuries after Plato, in a very different philosophical idiom (Neoplatonism), but the text is placed here because it directly revises the Platonic account.

**The key move**: Plotinus explicitly rejects the standard classical theory that beauty is a matter of **symmetry/proportion of parts** (the dominant Greco-Roman aesthetic theory, associated with Polyclitus's *Canon* and widespread in rhetorical and visual theory). His argument: if beauty were just proportion, only composite things (things with parts) could be beautiful — but we also find single, simple things beautiful (a single color, a single musical note, virtue itself, which has no "parts" to be proportioned). So beauty can't reduce to proportion.

Instead, Plotinus argues beauty is the presence of **Form/intelligible unity actively organizing and illuminating matter** — a beautiful object is one where soul/intellect has successfully imposed unity on otherwise formless matter, making it "share in reason." Ugliness, correspondingly, isn't disproportion — it's matter that has resisted or escaped the ordering power of form, remaining recalcitrant and unshaped.

**Why this matters for the unit**: Plotinus takes the ladder-structure from the *Symposium* (ascent from body to soul to intellect to the One) but relocates the *criterion* of beauty away from proportion/measurement and toward something closer to a metaphysics of active organization versus passive resistance. This has real consequences for thinking about bodies specifically — on Plotinus's account, a body's beauty is a matter of how successfully it's been *taken over* by soul/form, not a matter of its measurements. The body is a site where an ordering force and an unruly substrate are in tension, and beauty is legible as the visible record of which one is currently winning.

## Comparative/throughline notes for comps

- **Three different eros/embodiment accounts on one syllabus day**, and the differences are sharper than they first look: *Phaedrus* keeps eros anchored in a specific body throughout, with real physiological stakes for the lover; the *Symposium* ladder treats the specific body as a disposable starting point to be abstracted past; Plotinus relocates beauty away from bodies-with-proportions altogether, toward form successfully governing matter — which makes the body a site of *contest* between form and its own resistant materiality, rather than either an object of sustained desire (*Phaedrus*) or a rung to climb past (*Symposium*).
- **This is probably the single most useful cluster on the whole syllabus for your own research.** The Plotinian model — beauty as the visible record of form's success in organizing/subduing an unruly bodily substrate — maps with unusual precision onto how theatrical and literary traditions describe successful cross-dressing or gender performance: not as proportion or resemblance to an external ideal, but as the visible sign that a performative discipline (training, gesture, costume, voice) has successfully taken hold of and reorganized the body. A "failed" or "read" performance, on this analogy, is exactly Plotinus's ugliness — matter that has resisted or slipped out from under the organizing form. Worth testing whether this gives you a genuinely useful vocabulary (form vs. recalcitrant matter) that's more precise than "success/failure" for describing what commentators like Zhang Zhupo or the Chongzhen annotator are actually praising or condemning in scenes of dress, gesture, and self-presentation.
- **The Symposium ladder's instrumentalization of the particular beloved** is worth setting directly against Butler: where Diotima's structure treats the move from particular to universal as *ascent* (a achievement, a philosophical education), Butler's performativity has no such hierarchy — there is no "beyond" the particular repeated act to ascend to, no Form waiting at the top that the citation is imperfectly approximating. Framing it this way gives you a clean philosophical contrast to open a comps answer with: Platonic eros is teleological and hierarchical (particular instances exist to be transcended); performativity is anti-teleological (there is no non-particular instance to arrive at).

## Questions to bring to seminar / hold for comps

1. Is the *Symposium* ladder's move from particular body to abstract Form actually compatible with the *Phaedrus*'s insistence that the beloved's specific face and body remain present through the whole process of recollection — or are these simply two different, not fully reconcilable theories of what eros is *for*?
2. Does Plotinus's rejection of proportion-as-beauty solve a real problem in classical aesthetics, or does it just relocate the difficulty (now you need a theory of what counts as "successful" formal organization, which risks smuggling proportion-language back in through the side door)?
3. If beauty, for Plotinus, is legible as form's success against recalcitrant matter — what does that imply about bodies that are read as neither straightforwardly "formed" nor "unformed," but ambiguous or in-between (as in performance, disguise, or transition)? Does the framework have room for productive ambiguity, or does it force a binary judgment?
