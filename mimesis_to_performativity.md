# From Mimesis to Performativity: A Four-Way Comparative Sketch

*Working notes for classics-124-poetics — organized around Classics 124 (Ferrari, Berkeley, Spring 2016).*

## The shared question

Each of these four accounts asks the same underlying question: does representation *copy* a pre-existing reality, or does it *produce* the categories it appears only to describe? The through-line from Plato to Butler is a slow migration from the first answer to the second — and it's this migration that the comps argument extends into Ming/Qing commentarial practice.

## 1. Plato — mimesis as copy (and its instability)

The *Republic*'s official position treats mimesis as a copy at two removes from the Form, and worries about poetry's power to shape the soul of the audience through repeated imitation of bad models. But the worry itself concedes the constitutive point: if mimesis were merely descriptive, its moral danger would be inert. The anxiety about poetry's effect on character is already an admission that representation acts on, rather than merely reflects, the one who encounters it.

## 2. Aristotle — character through action

The *Poetics* relocates character (*ēthos*) from a pre-existing trait to something disclosed and, more strongly, *constituted* through action and choice (*prohairesis*) within the plot. Character is not represented so much as it is enacted into legibility by the sequence of a life under pressure of circumstance — a move that anticipates the performative claim that identity categories are effects of iterated acts rather than their expressive origin.

## 3. Longinus — sublimity as emulative self-formation

*On the Sublime* treats the encounter with a sublime predecessor text as producing the reader's own capacity for greatness — emulation (*zēlōsis*) doesn't just imitate a model, it forms the imitator into a subject capable of the greatness being imitated. The critical frame (what counts as sublime, whose greatness is worth emulating) is doing constitutive work on the reader's self-conception, not simply cataloguing pre-existing rhetorical features.

## 4. Butler — citationality

Gender is not expressed by acts but constituted through their reiteration; the "interior" identity the act appears to express is retroactively produced by the citational chain of acts themselves. The frame (the norm being cited) and the object it reads (the gendered subject) are not independent — the frame produces what it claims only to recognize.

## Why this matters for the comps argument

All four moments can be read as instances of the same structural problem the repo's throughline is built around: an interpretive or representational frame that presents itself as neutral or descriptive while actually generating the categories — moral character, sublime greatness, gendered coherence — that it claims only to find. This is the same move a sustained reading of Zhang Zhupo's commentary on *Jin Ping Mei* needs to make: the commentary's moral-didactic frame doesn't just interpret the novel's transgressive bodies, it produces the categories (chaste/licentious, masculine/feminine) that organize how those bodies are read.
