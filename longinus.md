# [Pseudo-]Longinus — On the Sublime

**Primary text:** *Peri Hypsous* (*On the Sublime*), esp. chs. 1, 8–9, 33–36.

## The argument

Longinus shifts the register of the debate from *whether* transgressive or excessive representation should be permitted to *when greatness excuses fault*.

1. **The sublime as transport, not persuasion (ch. 1).** Great writing does not merely convince an audience — it produces ekstasis, a self-forgetting elevation. This is explicitly contrasted with rhetorical persuasion, which leaves judgment intact.
2. **Five sources of sublimity (ch. 8)** — great thoughts, strong emotion, figures of speech, noble diction, and dignified word arrangement. Notably, "great thoughts" and "strong emotion" are treated as partly a matter of the author's inner greatness of soul, not technique alone.
3. **The genius exemption (ch. 33–36).** Longinus explicitly argues that a flawed work by a great genius (Homer nodding, Sophocles's excesses) is superior to a flawless minor work — greatness in the whole excuses particular faults. "A high soul's greatness, even where it falls short, still leaves us in admiration."

## The external frame

This is the boldest version of the move this repository tracks: Longinus makes the license for transgression *explicitly* a function of an external judgment about the author's greatness of soul, rather than anything internal to the passage itself. The same graphic or excessive content is condemnable in a minor writer and sublime in Homer — the frame (genius) is doing all the moral work. This has an almost exact structural analogue in the commentarial tradition's argument that *Jin Ping Mei*'s author (identified in various theories, none certain) must have been a moralist of the highest order for the novel's excess to be legible as edifying rather than merely pornographic — the assumed genius/moral seriousness of the (anonymous) author licenses the content.

## Connection point

Longinus is the strongest Western precedent for reading Zhang Zhupo's prefatory insistence on the *implied author's* moral seriousness (as opposed to anything provable from the text alone) as doing genuine argumentative work rather than being mere rhetorical throat-clearing.
