# Cross-synthesis: Western poetics, the *Jin Ping Mei* commentarial tradition, and performativity

## Thesis

Defenses of transgressive representation — across Western poetics (Plato through neoclassicism) and the Ming-Qing Chinese commentarial tradition on *Jin Ping Mei* alike — consistently locate their justification in an interpretive frame external to the text itself. That frame does not merely describe the moral and gender categories it invokes to license or condemn the representation; following Judith Butler's account of performativity, it *produces* those categories through the very act of repeated framing.

## The comparative table

| Tradition | Text under judgment | External frame | What the frame produces |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plato | Mimetic poetry | The ideal city's pedagogical project | "Harmful representation" as a civic category |
| Aristotle | Tragic violence | Formal plot structure | "Beneficial catharsis" as a structural effect |
| Longinus | Excess and fault | Authorial genius | "Sublime" as exemption from ordinary fault |
| Sidney | Morally troubling content | Right poets / bad readers | "Corruption" relocated outside the art itself |
| Neoclassicism | Onstage transgression | Codified decorum | "Impropriety" as a pre-existing rule violation |
| Zhang Zhupo | Sexual/financial excess in *Jin Ping Mei* | Structural parallelism, authorial moral seriousness | "Warning" (懲戒) as the novel's constituted meaning |
| Naifei Ding | The category of obscenity itself | Discursive/critical history | "Obscenity" as a historically produced classification, not a textual given |
| Butler | Gendered acts and identities | Regulatory discourse (medical, legal, cultural) | "Natural" sex/gender as a retroactively naturalized effect of repetition |

## Why this comparison is not merely decorative

The temptation in comparative work of this kind is to treat the Western sequence as a set of theories and the Chinese material as a case study to which the theories get "applied." This project resists that hierarchy in two ways:

1. **Zhang Zhupo is treated as a theorist in his own right**, with his own account of how commentary constitutes textual meaning — not simply as a native informant whose reading needs a Western vocabulary to become legible as theory.
2. **Butler and Ding are treated as already having made a version of this argument** — the project's contribution is showing that the *same* structural pattern recurs across all these otherwise disconnected traditions, not importing Butler as an external explanatory tool applied one-directionally to Chinese material.

## The recension problem as a test case for the whole method

The Roy/Zhang Zhupo recension mismatch (see `jin-ping-mei/roy-translation-recension-crux.md`) is the sharpest available illustration of the broader thesis: it is a concrete, textually verifiable instance of a frame (Zhang's commentary, built for the *xiuxiang* text) being layered onto a base (Roy's *cihua*-based translation) it wasn't built for — and of readers, including automated ones, treating the resulting composite as if it simply described "what the novel means," rather than recognizing it as a constructed overlay. This is why the project's proposed benchmark (`benchmark-design.md`) uses recension-detection as an operationalizable proxy for the more abstract claim about frames producing rather than describing meaning.

## Open threads for future work

- Anastasia-Erasmia Peponi's *Frontiers of Pleasure* as a possible bridge between the Western poetics unit's pleasure/danger debates and Ding's discourse-analytic treatment of obscenity.
- Kris Ercums's work on Ming Wong as a contemporary art-historical counterpoint.
- Anna Wing Bo Tso's work on cross-dressing in Chinese literary classics, for situating the project's Butlerian reading within existing Sinophone criticism rather than presenting it as an import.
