# Sir Philip Sidney — An Apology for Poetry

**Primary text:** *The Defence of Poesy* / *An Apology for Poetry* (c. 1580s).

## The argument

Sidney's *Apology* is a direct Renaissance response to Puritan attacks on poetry (Stephen Gosson's *The School of Abuse*), and self-consciously restages the Plato/Aristotle debate for an English Protestant context.

1. **Poetry as "golden world" over nature's "brazen" one.** The poet is not bound by what is (the historian) or what must be proven (the philosopher) — the poet ranges "within the zodiac of his own wit" and can present better models than nature actually offers: a Cyrus more perfect than the historical Cyrus.
2. **Teaching through delight.** Sidney's central formula: poetry teaches *and* delights, and teaches better than philosophy because it moves as well as instructs — the philosopher gives the precept, the poet gives the example that makes the precept felt.
3. **Answering the three charges.** Sidney systematically answers accusations that poetry (a) wastes time better spent on more fruitful knowledge, (b) is "the mother of lies," and (c) corrupts morals — the last is the one most relevant here: Sidney's defense is that poetry does not corrupt, "abuse" corrupts poetry; the fault lies with bad poets and bad readers, not the art itself.

## The external frame

Sidney's move is to relocate potential harm outside the text entirely — onto the poet's skill or the reader's misuse — while crediting all benefit to the art's essential nature ("right poets"). This is a defensive strategy structurally opposite in direction but identical in *form* to Longinus: instead of an external frame (genius) licensing transgressive content, an external frame (bad poets, bad readers) is blamed for transgression that the art itself supposedly never produces. Either way, the text's moral status is not read off the text — it is assigned by an interpretive apparatus standing outside it.

## Connection point

Compare to the recurring move in *Jin Ping Mei* commentary (both Zhang Zhupo and later apologists) that "vulgar readers" (俗人) misread the novel as pornography while the properly attuned reader (知音, "one who knows the tone") sees its moral seriousness. The category of reader is doing the same work Sidney assigns to the category of poet: the text's status is contingent on who is doing the reading, not fixed by the text.
