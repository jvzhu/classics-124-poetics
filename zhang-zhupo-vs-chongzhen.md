# Zhang Zhupo vs. the Chongzhen Commentary on *Jin Ping Mei*

*Two commentarial traditions on one novel: the anonymous late-Ming Chongzhen-era commentary, and Zhang Zhupo's 1695 edition.*

## The two traditions, briefly

**Chongzhen commentary** — anonymous, attached to the *Chongzhen* recension of the novel (named for the reign period, 1628–1644), predates Zhang Zhupo by roughly half a century and reflects late-Ming reading practice, closer in spirit to the more permissive, feeling-centered literary culture associated with figures like Li Zhi and the Gong'an school.

**Zhang Zhupo (1670–1698)** — failed the imperial examinations five times before turning to editing and annotating fiction; his 1695 commentary edition became the standard text through which the novel was read for the next two and a half centuries, and remains the edition David Roy's and Andrew Plaks's influential English-language scholarship leans on most heavily.

## The defense-of-content problem, and Zhang's answer

Both commentators inherit the same basic problem Sidney faces in the English tradition and Plutarch faces even earlier: a text contains extensive, potentially corrupting content (explicit sex, graphic violence, moral transgression), and a reader needs some framework that keeps engagement with that content from being simple indulgence.

Zhang's answer, reported directly in his own commentary: readers who find the novel pornographic have "read only the pornographic passages" — the fault is a failure of reading practice, not a property of the text. He grounds the deeper defense in **Xunzi's philosophy of human nature as evil** (in contrast to the Mencian/Confucian mainstream view of human nature as basically good) — the novel's unflinching depiction of depravity is not indulgence but **diagnosis**, consistent with a philosophical tradition that says accurate depiction of vice is the necessary first step toward the ritual/moral correction Xunzi's system prescribes.

Zhang doesn't stop at argument — he builds an actual **reading method**: sustained line-by-line explication, attention to structural echo and foreshadowing across the novel's hundred chapters, and an explicit claim to authority as the interpreter who can show you how to read correctly. This is the same move Plutarch makes in miniature (train the reader in real time) and the same move Augustine makes structurally (bound interpretation by an external rule) — except Zhang fuses both into a single totalizing apparatus applied to one novel.

## Where the two commentaries diverge — Wu Song and Pan Jinlian

Chapter 2's failed seduction scene (Pan Jinlian propositions Wu Song; he violently rejects her) is the clearest test case for how differently the two traditions read the same material.

**Chongzhen commentator**: reads *against* the novel's ostensible moral hierarchy — criticizes Wu Song's rejection as the crude behavior of a rigid neo-Confucian moralist (*daoxue xiansheng*), explicitly using the phrase that his response doesn't approach or accord with ordinary human feeling (*bujin renqing*). Elsewhere in the commentary, the same annotator notes that even Pan Jinlian has genuine feelings of pleasure and frustration and simply wants to be loved — treating her interiority as continuous with recognizable human emotion, not as a moral case study to be diagnosed.

**Zhang Zhupo**: applies a single ritual-propriety standard uniformly across the novel — strict enough that he criticizes even conventionally virtuous characters (Wu Yueniang, the principal wife) for ritual missteps. This has a leveling effect: Pan Jinlian isn't singled out for unique condemnation so much as she's caught in a totalizing grid that has no category for "sympathetic, comprehensible desire" to begin with — every character, good or bad, is measured against the same ritual-correctness standard.

**The consequence**: the same scene, same character, same lines of dialogue produce two structurally opposed readings of what Pan Jinlian's desire *is* — psychologically real and worth inhabiting (Chongzhen) vs. a ritual infraction requiring diagnosis (Zhang). Neither commentator is reporting a fact about the text that exists independent of their reading; each commentary is, in an important sense, producing the gender politics it claims only to be explaining.

## Clothing as the mechanism, not cross-dressing

There isn't a sustained cross-dressing plot in *Jin Ping Mei* comparable to, say, Ming drama's stock disguise plots — but clothing does comparable performative work. Scholarship on the novel's clothing economy argues Pan Jinlian uses **control of dress and undress as leverage** in an ongoing status competition with Ximen Qing's other wives and concubines, rather than as an expression of a fixed, already-existing self. Clothing here is not reporting an interior truth; it's a repeated, strategic act that *constitutes* her position in the household in real time, chapter by chapter.

Zhang Zhupo's annotations on parallel material — Li Ping'er's gift-giving of shoes and hairpins to smooth over a discovered affair — tend to flag these material objects as evidence of superficiality and manipulation, pinning the gesture back to a stable claim about the character's essential deceitfulness. Where the clothing/gift economy in the text itself behaves in a genuinely performative way (status and desire produced in the act, not behind it), Zhang's commentary consistently resists that logic in favor of an essentialist one — the object is made to point back to a fixed moral truth about who the character *really is*.

## Working thesis for this file

The Chongzhen/Zhang Zhupo split isn't simply "sympathetic Ming reading vs. harsh Qing reading" as a matter of temperament — it's traceable to two different underlying theories of what a commentary is *for*. Chongzhen's annotator seems closer to Plutarch's model (attentiveness, real-time correction, feeling taken seriously as evidence) without a rigid external rule; Zhang is closer to Augustine's model (an external, totalizing framework — Xunzian moral anthropology, Confucian ritual propriety — that determines which readings are legitimate) but without Augustine's willingness to let *multiple* readings stand as simultaneously true. Zhang wants one correct reading, enforced by one rule, applied uniformly — which is precisely what turns his commentary into the site Ding Naifei identifies as manufacturing, rather than merely reporting, the novel's apparent misogyny.

## Open questions to carry into the Ding Naifei file

1. Is the Chongzhen commentator's sympathy for Pan Jinlian itself uncomplicated, or does treating her interiority as "recognizable human feeling" carry its own risks of naturalizing or excusing content that deserves harder scrutiny?
2. Zhang's ritual-propriety standard is applied to *all* characters, not just women — does that uniformity actually protect against misogynistic reading, or does its formal evenhandedness disguise a deeper asymmetry in how ritual infractions by women vs. men get weighted?
3. How much of the difference between the two commentaries is genuinely about Pan Jinlian specifically, versus a broader shift in what counts as legitimate literary-critical authority between the late Ming and early Qing?
