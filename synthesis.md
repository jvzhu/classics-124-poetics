# Synthesis: The Arc of Classics 124

*A study-guide index across all seven note files, tracing the throughline from mimesis to interpretation to medium.*

## The shape of the argument, syllabus-wide

Read end to end, the syllabus tells a story about what happens to a single problem — **how does representation relate to truth, and who gets to say what a representation means or does?** — as it's handed off across roughly two thousand years and multiple genres of writing (philosophical dialogue, technical treatise, rhetorical handbook, scriptural exegesis, dramatist's apologia, verse essay, philosophical essay, art criticism).

The arc runs through three broad phases:

### Phase 1 — What is mimesis, and is it dangerous? (Plato, Aristotle)
Plato treats mimesis as either possession without knowledge (*Ion*) or metaphysical/psychological danger (*Republic* 2–3, 10) — poetry copies appearances, not truth, and habituates the soul toward whatever it imitates. Aristotle answers by relocating mimesis from metaphysical demotion to cognitive achievement: poetry deals in probable/necessary universals, which makes it "more philosophical" than history, and its plot mechanics (*hamartia*, reversal, recognition) are engineered specifically to produce pity and fear that *katharsis* discharges rather than corrupts. → **`plato-aristotle-notes.md`**, **`aristotle-plot-mechanics-notes.md`**

### Phase 2 — What licenses reading transgressive or overwhelming content well? (Longinus, beauty/eros cluster, Plutarch, Augustine)
Longinus adds a third model of engagement with a text — neither Platonic possession nor Aristotelian rule-following, but agonistic emulation, where flawed greatness outranks flawless mediocrity. The beauty/eros cluster (*Phaedrus*, *Symposium*, Plotinus) works out what erotic/bodily response to beauty is *for* — recollection, ascent past the particular, or the visible record of form organizing matter. Plutarch and Augustine then tackle the practical problem directly: given that readers will encounter morally mixed or richly ambiguous texts, what disciplines a legitimate reading? Plutarch trains the individual reader in real time; Augustine licenses **multiple simultaneously true readings**, bounded by an external rule (faith, charity) rather than a single correct meaning. → **`longinus-notes.md`**, **`beauty-eros-notes.md`**, **`plutarch-augustine-notes.md`**

### Phase 3 — Sidney's hinge, and the fracture into medium- and judge-relative standards (Sidney, Corneille/Pope/Hume/Lessing)
Sidney sits at the pivot: his "golden world" quietly abandons mimesis-as-copying (siding, unacknowledged, with Aristotle's anti-Platonic move) while retaining a strongly intentionalist, moralizing defense structure closer to Augustine's rule-bound pluralism than to Plutarch's reader-training. His refutation of the "nurse of abuse" charge relocates blame onto bad readers without ever building the mechanism Plutarch or Zhang Zhupo bother to construct. After Sidney, the tradition fractures for good: Corneille locates authority in professional practice and audience effect, Hume relocates it in the converging judgment of qualified critics, and Lessing insists representation can't be theorized as one thing at all once you take medium (space vs. time) seriously. → **`sidney-discussion-notes.md`**, **`corneille-pope-hume-lessing-notes.md`**

## Cross-cutting threads worth having ready for comps

**1. The mimesis/craft/possession triangle** — track how every later text implicitly positions itself relative to Plato's possession model (*Ion*) and Aristotle's craft model (*Poetics*). Longinus adds emulation; Sidney's "maker" tries to have both possession-adjacent transcendence and craft-adjacent intentional design at once.

**2. Interpretive authority — who gets to fix a text's meaning, and how** — Augustine (doctrinal rule) → Plutarch (trained reader) → Hume (converging qualified judges) → Zhang Zhupo / Chongzhen commentator (competing external frameworks, ritual vs. sympathetic). This thread is your strongest bridge into the *Jin Ping Mei* material, since it lets you show the commentarial split isn't a modern or culturally specific anomaly but an instance of a much older, recurring problem in how traditions license multiple true (or competing) readings of one text.

**3. The body as the site where theory gets tested** — *Republic* 3's worry about impersonation reshaping the actor; the *Phaedrus*'s physiological account of erotic response; Plotinus's beauty-as-form-organizing-matter; Lessing's argument that a body's suffering must be staged differently depending on medium. Each of these gives you a distinct, historically specific vocabulary for talking about embodiment and representation — worth deciding, for any given comps question, which vocabulary is doing the most precise work rather than defaulting to modern performativity language alone.

**4. Genre of the text itself as evidence** — notice that the *kind* of text carries argumentative weight: philosophical dialogue (Plato) vs. technical treatise (Aristotle) vs. rhetorical handbook (Longinus) vs. scriptural exegesis (Augustine) vs. practicing dramatist's apologia (Corneille) vs. verse essay (Pope) vs. philosophical essay (Hume) vs. art criticism occasioned by a specific sculpture (Lessing). A text's genre is itself already a claim about what kind of authority is licensed to speak about poetry — worth having as a meta-observation for any comps answer that risks flattening these into a single undifferentiated "tradition."
