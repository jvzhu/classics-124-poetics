# Naifei Ding, *Obscene Things* (Duke UP, 2002)

## Position in the field

Ding's monograph is the dominant English-language critical treatment of *Jin Ping Mei*'s sexual content. Rather than adjudicating whether the novel "is" pornographic or moral (the question the Ming-Qing commentarial tradition itself repeatedly poses), Ding treats the *category* of obscenity as historically and discursively produced — obscenity is not a property of the text but an effect of the reading practices, commentarial traditions, and censorship regimes that have accreted around it.

## Relevance to the external-frame thesis

Ding's move is structurally continuous with — and in some ways anticipates — this project's own thesis: she is already arguing that the moral/sexual categories applied to the novel are produced by the apparatus surrounding it (commentary, censorship history, translation choices) rather than discovered within the text. This makes Ding less a straightforward source to synthesize *into* the Zhang Zhupo / Western poetics comparison and more a **methodological precedent** — someone already doing a version of the Butlerian move (categories as produced by repeated framing acts, not prior to them) with respect to obscenity specifically.

## Where this project extends Ding

1. **Recension-specificity.** Ding's analysis, like most Anglophone criticism, tends to treat "*Jin Ping Mei*" as a single stable object. This project's insistence on distinguishing *cihua* from *xiuxiang*, and Zhang Zhupo's commentary from the text it was and wasn't written for, adds a textual-historical layer of precision that Ding's discourse-analytic approach doesn't foreground.
2. **Cross-traditional comparison.** Ding's argument is developed primarily within Chinese literary and critical history (plus Foucauldian/discourse-analytic theory). This project puts her discursive-construction thesis in direct dialogue with the Western poetics sequence (Plato through neoclassicism) and with Butler's performativity — testing whether "obscenity as discursively produced" (Ding) and "gender/morality as performatively produced" (Butler) are doing structurally the same kind of theoretical work.
3. **Cross-dressing as an unclaimed gap.** Ding's focus is chiefly on sexual explicitness and its discursive management; a sustained Butlerian reading specifically of cross-dressing and gender performance within the novel is not, on current review, a settled feature of her argument or of the wider Anglophone secondary literature — flagged here as an open opportunity rather than a claim about what Ding does or doesn't cover exhaustively.

## Adjacent connection points

- Anastasia-Erasmia Peponi, *Frontiers of Pleasure* (Oxford UP, 2012) — relevant for a theorized account of aesthetic pleasure that could bridge Ding's discourse-analytic obscenity and the Western poetics sequence's pleasure/danger debates (Plato's psychagogic risk, Aristotle's catharsis).
- Kris Ercums's work on Ming Wong's *Next Year/L'Année Prochaine* — adjacent via queer theory and performativity, useful as a contemporary art-historical counterpoint to a purely textual argument.
- Anna Wing Bo Tso's work on female cross-dressing in Chinese literary classics — a scholarly connection point for situating any cross-dressing argument within existing Sinophone gender-and-genre criticism, rather than presenting it as importing Butler wholesale from outside.
