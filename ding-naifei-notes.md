# Naifei Ding, *Obscene Things: The Sexual Politics of the Jin Ping Mei* (Duke UP, 2002)

*Companion file to `zhang-zhupo-vs-chongzhen.md`. Ding's critique targets the commentarial tradition itself, not primarily the novel.*

## The core thesis

Ding's target isn't the novel's content — it's the **critical tradition that has read the novel for three centuries**. Her claim: that tradition is unified by a shared move, traceable to Zhang Zhupo's 1695 commentary, that equates *woman* with *sex and evil*, crystallized in the figure of the ***yinfu*** ("lascivious woman"), with Pan Jinlian as its ultimate embodiment. This isn't a neutral description the commentary discovers in the text — Ding argues Zhang's reading strategies actively **manufacture** this equation, and that the manufactured reading has proven remarkably durable, shaping not just subsequent scholarship but the modern Chinese cultural imagination's sense of who Pan Jinlian *is*.

## The materialist move — Jinlian as bondmaid-concubine, not abstraction

Ding's sharpest and most citable point: Pan Jinlian is not, in the novel's own material terms, an abstraction of "Woman" — she is first and specifically a **bondmaid-concubine** (a status category, not just a marital one), a figure representing an increasingly common and precarious class position in the late-Ming market economy, where poor families sold or indentured daughters into service, and service could shade into concubinage. Zhang's commentary, in Ding's account, performs an **erasure of this material, economic, status-specific reality**, replacing a woman's structurally exploited position with an ahistorical moral-sexual essence (the "sexy murderer," desire as innate danger). The *yinfu* label isn't just an insult — it's a category substitution that removes class and labor from view and puts "female nature" in their place.

**Why this matters for comps**: this gives you a genuinely materialist companion to the more purely discursive/performative reading you'd get from a Butler-only frame. Ding isn't just saying the gender category is constructed through commentary (a performativity-adjacent point) — she's saying the construction does specific ideological work, covering over a class/labor reality that would otherwise implicate the household economy and the men who buy, sell, and circulate women within it.

## The complicity charge — extending to modern scholarship

Ding doesn't stop at Zhang Zhupo. She argues the charge extends to **modern aesthetic and formalist scholarship** that reads the novel primarily for literary coherence, structural craft, and narrative artistry — including, by clear implication, David Roy's and Andrew Plaks's influential English-language work, both of which lean heavily on Zhang Zhupo as an interpretive authority. On Ding's account, praising the novel's formal achievement without interrogating the misogynistic reading-structure inherited from Zhang doesn't escape the problem — it launders it, dressing up a specific historical erasure as timeless literary insight.

This is worth being precise about for comps: **Roy's "firm moral structure" reading** (retribution exacted for sexual libertinism, grounded in Xunzian human-nature-as-evil) is not just *compatible with* Zhang Zhupo's frame — Roy is substantially importing it wholesale, and Ding's critique lands on Roy as much as on Zhang, even though Roy is writing three centuries later and in an entirely different institutional context (Western sinology).

## Relation to the Chongzhen commentary

Ding's book is centrally about Zhang Zhupo, but the contrast with the earlier Chongzhen commentator (see companion file) sharpens her point rather than undermining it: the fact that an *earlier* commentarial tradition could read the same scenes with more sympathy for Jinlian's interiority shows the misogynistic reading isn't an inevitable property of the text itself — it's a specific, datable, attributable intervention that Zhang made and that later reception naturalized as simply "what the novel says." Ding's genealogical method depends on being able to point to a moment where the reading changed, which the Chongzhen/Zhang split supplies.

## Where this sits relative to a Butler/performativity frame

Ding's argument is adjacent to, but not identical with, a Butlerian performativity reading, and it's worth keeping the distinction sharp for comps:

- A performativity reading would emphasize that Jinlian's gender/desire has **no essence prior to its citational construction** in the text and its commentaries — meaning is made in the repeated act, not behind it.
- Ding's argument is more **genealogical and materialist**: she's less interested in showing gender is constructed-rather-than-essential in the abstract, and more interested in showing *this specific construction*, by *this specific commentator*, did *this specific ideological work* — erasing a bondmaid's economic condition in favor of an eternal "female danger" narrative that has served particular interests (moral, institutional, and later scholarly-aesthetic) for three hundred years.

The two frames are compatible but do different work: performativity theory gives you the general mechanism (meaning made in the act of reading/commentary, not prior to it); Ding gives you the historically specific instance, the identifiable agent (Zhang Zhupo), and the material stakes (class and labor erased in favor of moral-sexual essence) that a purely formal performativity account wouldn't surface on its own.

## Working thesis for the *Jin Ping Mei* folder as a whole

Put together with the Sidney/Zhang comparison already in the repo, the throughline across the whole folder is: **defense-of-transgressive-content rhetorics — whether Sidney's golden world, Zhang's Xunzian diagnosis, or Roy's inherited "firm moral structure" — are never neutral shields. They are productive machinery that manufactures the very gender and moral categories they claim only to be defending against misreading.** Ding supplies the sharpest, most historically specific version of this claim available in the material gathered here, and gives you a named target (Zhang Zhupo, and by extension Roy) rather than a diffuse claim about commentarial tradition in general.

## Reading note

This file is built from search-derived summaries of Ding's argument, not a direct reading of *Obscene Things* itself. The book's table of contents (noted separately) suggests Chapter 4 ("Tears of Resentment: Zhang Zhupo's *Jin Ping Mei*"), Chapter 5 ("Seduction: Tiger and *Yinfu*" — likely her direct treatment of the Wu Song/Jinlian scene), and Chapter 6 ("Red Shoes, Foot Bindings, and the Swing" — clothing/body politics) are the chapters most directly relevant to the material in this folder. Reading these directly before finalizing any comps answer built on Ding's argument is strongly advisable — secondhand summary risks flattening a genuinely dense, close-textual argument into a set of claims that are easy to cite but harder to defend under exam pressure without the primary support.

## Open questions

1. Does Ding's materialist critique (bondmaid-concubine status erased by moral-sexual essence) require the performativity frame at all, or does it stand as an independent, sufficient critique on its own terms?
2. If Roy's scholarship substantially reproduces Zhang Zhupo's frame, does that make Roy's translation itself a compromised object for teaching/citation — or is it possible to use Roy's philologically rigorous translation while explicitly bracketing his interpretive apparatus?
3. Ding's critique targets formalist/aesthetic readings as complicit — where, if anywhere, does a genuinely formalist reading of the novel's structure (its parallelism, its foreshadowing, its narrative architecture) escape this charge, or is Ding's claim that no purely formal reading can escape it?
