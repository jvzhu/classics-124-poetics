# Sample Comps Answer: Defenses of Transgressive Representation

*A worked example, written as if in response to a plausible comps prompt, drawing on all twelve files in this repo. Meant as a model for pacing, citation density, and argumentative structure under exam time pressure — not a finished, submittable answer.*

## Sample prompt

*"Compare at least two traditions' strategies for defending literature that depicts moral transgression. What do these defenses reveal about the relationship between representation and the categories (moral, gender, or otherwise) it claims only to describe?"*

## Sample answer (~1200 words, roughly a 45–50 minute exam pace)

Defenses of transgressive literary content share a recurring structure across otherwise unconnected traditions: a text depicts something morally troubling, critics charge it with corrupting its audience, and its defenders respond not by denying the content but by supplying an external frame that redeems it. Comparing Philip Sidney's *Defence of Poetry* (1580s) with Zhang Zhupo's 1695 commentary on *Jin Ping Mei* makes this structure visible with unusual clarity, because the two texts are historically unconnected, respond to structurally identical accusations, and arrive at nearly mirror-image defenses. Reading them together — and against the earlier commentarial tradition each is implicitly departing from — shows that these defensive frames do not merely protect transgressive content from misreading; they actively produce the moral and gender categories they claim only to explain.

**Sidney's defense.** Sidney answers the charge that poetry is a "nurse of abuse" by relocating fault onto bad poets and bad readers, not poetry itself. His deeper defense is metaphysical: the poet is a "maker" who produces a "golden world," exceeding nature's "brazen" one rather than merely copying it. This is a genuine departure from the mimesis-as-copying assumption inherited from the classical tradition — though Sidney's own rhetoric doesn't fully own how radical the move is, since he continues to use the vocabulary of imitation even as his actual claim (poetry's value lies in exceeding, not copying, reality) abandons mimesis as a ground of value. The defense is fundamentally intentionalist: transgressive content is redeemed because a sovereign authorial will has shaped it toward virtue. Sidney never builds a mechanism for producing the virtuous reader his argument requires — he simply assumes well-made poetry will move readers correctly, leaving the "nurse of abuse" charge only partially answered.

**Zhang Zhupo's defense.** Zhang faces an almost identical accusation — that *Jin Ping Mei*'s explicit content corrupts readers — and answers with a structurally inverted defense. Where Sidney's frame improves on nature, Zhang's diagnoses it: grounding his reading in Xunzi's philosophy of human nature as inherently evil, Zhang argues the novel's unflinching depiction of vice is not indulgence but accurate diagnosis, consistent with a philosophical tradition in which correction requires first seeing evil clearly. Critically, Zhang goes further than Sidney: rather than simply asserting readers will respond correctly, he constructs an actual reading method — sustained line-by-line explication and structural commentary — designed to produce the correct reader Sidney only assumes into existence. In this respect Zhang's defense is the more rigorous of the two, closer to Plutarch's earlier project of training a reader in real time than to Sidney's purely argumentative response.

**What the comparison reveals.** Despite their opposite content-claims (poetry as improvement vs. fiction as diagnosis), both defenses share the same underlying logic: transgressive content is licensed by an external, stable frame that the content itself is said to serve. And in both cases, that frame does more than defend the content — it silently authorizes whatever moral and gender order the frame assumes. This becomes visible once the *Jin Ping Mei* commentarial tradition is read alongside a genuinely different, earlier commentary on the same text. The anonymous Chongzhen-era commentator, writing roughly half a century before Zhang, reads the same novel's central female character, Pan Jinlian, very differently: where Zhang's Xunzian frame treats her desire as a case requiring ritual diagnosis, the Chongzhen commentator reads Wu Song's violent rejection of her as itself the crude behavior of a rigid moralist, and treats Jinlian's own feelings of desire and frustration as continuous with ordinary, recognizable human emotion. The same scene, the same lines of dialogue, produce two incompatible accounts of what Jinlian's desire *is*, depending entirely on which commentarial apparatus is laid over the text. Neither commentary is simply reporting a fact the novel already contains; each is producing the gender politics it claims only to be describing.

Naifei Ding's genealogical critique in *Obscene Things* sharpens this point considerably. Ding argues Zhang's frame does not merely misread Pan Jinlian — it actively manufactures her as an abstract "lascivious woman" (*yinfu*), erasing her actual, historically specific status as a bondmaid-concubine within the late-Ming household economy. This is a materialist claim, not simply a discursive one: the moralizing defense converts a woman's structurally exploited economic position into a timeless claim about female nature, and Ding extends the charge to modern scholarship, including David Roy's influential English translation, which imports Zhang's Xunzian frame nearly wholesale — his own "firm moral structure" reading of the novel is, in substance, Zhang's defense re-authorized by twentieth-century sinological credibility, rather than an independently argued position.

Judith Butler's account of gender performativity supplies the theoretical vocabulary for why this pattern recurs: gender, on Butler's argument, is not an inner truth a text or a body merely expresses, but an effect produced through repeated, citational acts — there is no subject or essence prior to the performance that the performance simply reports on. Zhang's commentary, read this way, is not discovering a preexisting truth about Pan Jinlian's nature; it is one iteration in a citational chain that produces her intelligibility as a specific *kind* of woman, a chain Ding traces forward into Roy's twentieth-century translation. Sidney's golden world performs an analogous operation in the opposite direction — not diagnosing evil but modeling improved virtue — but the underlying mechanism, an external frame producing rather than merely reflecting the categories it claims to describe, is the same.

The clearest exception in this material is the Chongzhen commentator, who does not obviously operate through any comparable totalizing frame at all — no Xunzian anthropology, no doctrinal rule, no golden world. The sympathy extended to Jinlian's desire reads, structurally, closer to Aristotle's account of tragic pity in the *Poetics* and *Rhetoric*: pity depends on a sufferer being similar enough to the observer to imagine their situation as one's own, which is arguably what the Chongzhen commentator is doing when treating Jinlian's frustration as legible, ordinary human feeling rather than a case to be diagnosed. Whether this represents a genuine alternative to the defensive-frame structure, or simply a different, less visible frame (treating emotional legibility itself as natural rather than constructed), is worth holding open — it may be the strongest available candidate in this material for a defense of transgressive content that doesn't reproduce the problem Ding identifies, but that claim itself deserves more scrutiny than a comps answer under time pressure can fully deliver.

**Conclusion.** Sidney and Zhang Zhupo, despite complete historical independence, produce structurally identical defenses of transgressive content — external frames that redeem rather than deny the content — and in both cases, per Ding's critique (extended through Butler's account of citational production), the frame does more than defend; it manufactures the moral and gender order it claims only to explain. The Chongzhen commentator's comparative frame-lessness is the one piece of evidence in this material suggesting an alternative is at least conceivable, though whether it constitutes a genuine escape from the pattern, or merely a subtler instance of it, remains an open question worth pursuing further.

## Notes on this sample for revision

- **Word/time budget**: ~1200 words fits a 45–50 minute slot in most quiz-format or short-essay comps formats; adjust up if your format allows a full class period per question.
- **What's doing the real argumentative work**: the taxonomy logic from `cross-synthesis.md` (external frame → naturalizes categories it claims to explain) is the spine. Everything else is evidence for or against that spine.
- **Where this answer is deliberately incomplete**: it doesn't use Longinus, Plotinus, Augustine, Plutarch, Hume, or Lessing at all — a different prompt (e.g., on interpretive authority specifically, or on medium) would pull different files forward. Don't force every file into every answer; let the prompt determine which 3–4 files are actually load-bearing.
- **The open question left standing at the end** (is the Chongzhen commentator's frame-lessness real or illusory) is intentional — a comps answer that closes every question it raises can read as less rigorous than one that identifies a genuine, still-open interpretive problem and says so plainly.
