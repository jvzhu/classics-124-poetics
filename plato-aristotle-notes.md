# Plato and Aristotle on Mimesis and Fictionality

*Covers: Plato,* Ion; *Phaedrus* 274b–278e; *Republic* 2–3, 10; *Philebus* 47d–50d • Aristotle, *Poetics* ch. 1–5, 9, 24–5*
Syllabus weeks: Jan. 19 – Feb. 18*

## I. Plato, *Ion* — poetry as possession, not craft

Socrates traps the rhapsode Ion in a dilemma: either reciting/interpreting Homer is a *technē* (a transferable skill, like medicine), in which case Ion should be equally good on every poet, not just Homer — or it isn't a skill at all, and Ion's excellence comes from something outside rational craft.

Plato's answer: poetic composition and performance are **divine possession** (*enthousiasmos*), transmitted like a magnet's force through a chain — Muse to poet to rhapsode to audience — none of whom understand what they're doing while it's happening.

**Why this matters for the unit:** this is Plato's first move against poetry, and it's not moral, it's epistemic. The poet has no *knowledge* of what he represents. This sets up everything Aristotle will later contest — Aristotle's whole *Poetics* can be read as arguing poetry *is* a craft, governed by intelligible rules (plot structure, probability, causation), against Plato's possession model.

**Discussion angle:** Ion is arguably a proto-performativity text — poetic authority is constituted in the act of transmission, not grounded in a stable prior competence. Worth flagging as a companion to Sidney's later, very different account of the poet as sovereign "maker" — Plato's poet is a conduit; Sidney's poet is closer to a god.

## II. *Phaedrus* 274b–278e — writing, memory, and the living word

This passage (the Theuth myth) is officially about writing vs. speech, not poetry directly, but it's on the syllabus here because it extends the *Ion* problem: written words, like the rhapsode, "always say only one and the same thing" — they can't defend themselves, explain themselves, or adjust to their audience. Living speech (dialectic) can.

**Connection point:** this is Plato's deepest worry about *fixed representation* generally — once something is externalized into a stable form (text, poem, image), it loses the responsiveness of a living, questioning mind. This is the conceptual root of the *Republic*'s anxiety about poetry as a fixed, uncorrectable influence on the soul.

## III. *Republic* 2–3 — the content censorship

Book 2–3 isn't about mimesis as a formal category yet — it's about **content**. Socrates proposes censoring the poets (Homer and Hesiod especially) because their stories misrepresent the gods as lying, quarreling, and causing harm, which will corrupt the guardians' souls by giving them false models to imitate.

Book 3 (392c ff.) then introduces the **formal** distinction that becomes crucial later: *diegesis* (plain narration, the poet speaking in his own voice) vs. *mimesis* (the poet speaking *as* a character, impersonating). Plato is suspicious of mimesis specifically because impersonation habituates the soul to becoming what it imitates — an actor who plays too many bad characters risks *becoming* morally unstable, on Plato's psychology.

**Key move to flag:** this is where "imitation" stops being a neutral descriptive term and becomes a technical, load-bearing concept with ethical stakes. Everything Aristotle does with *mimesis* in the *Poetics* is a direct response to this.

## IV. *Republic* 10 — the metaphysical demotion

The most famous passage in ancient poetics. Plato now gives mimesis a *metaphysical* location: the carpenter makes a bed (one remove from the Form of Bed); the painter paints a picture of a bed (two removes) — and the poet does the same kind of thing, representing appearances of virtue/action without knowledge of the reality behind them. Poetry is "third from the truth."

The chapter also revives the psychological argument from Book 3 in stronger form: even the best of us enjoy watching tragic grief onstage — pleasure we'd be ashamed of in real life. Poetry works on the "inferior part of the soul," the part susceptible to feeling rather than the rational part that should govern. This is the basis for banishing poets from the ideal city (with an exception carved out for hymns to gods and praise of good men).

**Discussion angle:** Plato's tripartite-soul argument in Book 10 is doing real psychological work that Aristotle's *katharsis* concept (via the *Poetics* and *Politics* 8, read later in the syllabus) is specifically built to answer — Aristotle grants that tragedy stirs pity and fear, but reframes the stirring as a *purgation/clarification*, not corruption.

## V. *Philebus* 47d–50d — the pleasure of comedy and mixed feelings

A shorter, less famous passage, but useful: Plato analyzes comic pleasure as a mixture of pleasure and pain — we enjoy laughing at a self-ignorant character's misfortune, which is itself a kind of malice (*phthonos*) mixed into the pleasure. This complicates the simple "poetry stirs bad feeling" claim from *Republic* 10 by showing Plato is capable of analyzing aesthetic response with more psychological granularity than the censorship argument alone suggests.

## VI. Aristotle, *Poetics* ch. 1–5, 9, 24–5 — mimesis rehabilitated

Aristotle takes Plato's loaded term and drains the anxiety out of it. Mimesis is simply **how humans learn** — ch. 4 states that imitation is natural to human beings from childhood, and that we take pleasure in accurate representations *even of things painful to see in reality* (a corpse, in a painting), because recognition and understanding are pleasurable in themselves. This directly answers *Republic* 10's worry about enjoying painful representations — for Aristotle, the pleasure is cognitive, not just cathartic indulgence.

**Ch. 9 — the famous poetry/history distinction:** history tells you what Alcibiades did; poetry tells you what a person *of a certain kind* would probably or necessarily do. Poetry is "more philosophical and more serious" than history because it deals with the universal, not the merely particular.

**Ch. 24–5:** extends mimesis/plausibility concerns to epic, and addresses objections to poetic "errors" (including outright impossibilities) — Aristotle's line is that a **convincing impossibility** is preferable to an unconvincing possibility. Internal coherence matters more than literal accuracy.

**The big reversal to flag:** where Plato treats mimesis as a metaphysical demotion (copy of a copy, epistemically empty), Aristotle treats it as an epistemically *productive* activity — poetic universals give you something philosophy-adjacent that history can't. This exact move — poetry as more philosophically serious than mere fact — is precisely what Sidney will redeploy centuries later in the poetry/philosophy/history triangulation you already have notes on.

## Comparative/throughline notes for comps

- **Ion → Poetics:** the arc from "poet has no knowledge, is possessed" to "poet has a craft, understands universals" is the single biggest hinge in the whole semester. Track how much of later poetics (Sidney included) is implicitly siding with Aristotle's craft-model while still needing *some* account of inspiration/genius to explain excellence — Sidney's language of the poet as quasi-divine "maker" is arguably an attempt to have both: craft *and* transcendence.
- **Republic 3's diegesis/mimesis split** is worth holding onto for your own work — the anxiety that *playing* a character (impersonation, cross-dressing included) reshapes the person doing the playing is structurally identical to later anxieties (Sidney-adjacent English anti-theatrical polemic, and Ming/Qing anxieties about actors/performers) about theatrical embodiment bleeding into real identity. Plato is arguably the origin point of a worry that recurs, transformed, in *every* tradition on this syllabus and in your own research.
- **Aristotle's universal/particular distinction** (ch. 9) is a useful analytic tool to bring into the Zhang Zhupo material: Zhang's defense of *Jin Ping Mei* claims the novel shows something *true about human nature generally* (Xunzian evil), which is structurally an Aristotelian move — particular depraved acts redeemed by universal moral instruction — even though Zhang never read Aristotle. Worth asking in comps prep whether that's a coincidence of moral-philosophical logic or a deeper structural necessity for any defense of transgressive mimetic content.

## Questions to bring to seminar / hold for comps

1. Is Aristotle's answer to Plato a genuine refutation, or does it just relocate the same anxiety (poetry shaping the soul) onto more comfortable ground (universals, cognitive pleasure)?
2. Does the diegesis/mimesis distinction in *Republic* 3 survive contact with drama, where *all* dialogue is technically mimesis? What does Plato's own use of dialogue form do to his argument?
3. If pleasure-in-recognition (*Poetics* ch. 4) is the basis of poetic pleasure, what happens to genres — like much of *Jin Ping Mei*, or explicit tragedy — where recognition is disturbing rather than merely informative?
