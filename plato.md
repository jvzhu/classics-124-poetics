# Plato — the expulsion of the poets

**Primary texts:** *Republic* Books II–III, X; *Ion*; *Phaedrus* 245a (the "divine madness" passage, in tension with the *Republic*'s hostility).

## The argument

Plato's case against poetry in the *Republic* is not really an aesthetic objection — it is a pedagogical and metaphysical one. Poetry is dangerous because:

1. **Ontological distance.** Mimetic art (Book X) is an imitation of an imitation — twice removed from the Forms. The poet does not know the truth of what he represents (the couch-maker's couch, the general's strategy) but merely reproduces its appearance.
2. **Psychagogic risk.** Poetry works on the irrational part of the soul. It bypasses reason and feeds the emotions directly, especially through the pleasure of mimetic identification (Book X's account of the spectator "giving way" to grief he would be ashamed of in his own life).
3. **Civic harm.** Because the guardians' souls are being *formed*, not merely informed, exposure to bad models (gods behaving badly, heroes lamenting) risks habituating the wrong dispositions before reason is mature enough to correct them.

## The external frame

Plato's justification for censorship is not "this content is bad" in isolation — it is bad *relative to* the ideal city's need to produce a certain kind of citizen-soul. The moral category ("harmful representation") is generated by the pedagogical project, not discovered independently of it. This is the first instance, in the Western sequence, of the move this repository tracks: **the frame that judges the representation is also what manufactures the standard by which it is judged.**

## Tension to flag

The *Ion* and *Phaedrus* complicate the *Republic*'s hostility — poetic inspiration there is treated as a divine possession, not a craft-failure. Any synthesis should not flatten Plato into a simple poetry-hater; the *Republic*'s position is a civic-pedagogical argument specific to the ideal city, not a universal aesthetic verdict.

## Connection point
