# Longinus, *On the Sublime* — Working Notes (Havell translation, 1890)

*Source: H. L. Havell's translation, with introduction by Andrew Lang (Macmillan, 1890) — public domain. Text via Project Gutenberg (EBook #17957).*

*Working notes for classics-124-poetics, focused on the emulation/self-formation material relevant to* mimesis_to_performativity.md.

## Chapter XIII — Emulation as a fifth source of sublimity

Longinus, having discussed the first four sources of sublimity, turns to Plato's own grandeur as an example of "gentle and noiseless" eloquence, quoting the *Republic* passage on souls who never rise above bodily appetite. He then names a further path to sublimity beyond the five formally enumerated: **the emulous imitation of the great writers of the past**.

Key passage (Longinus's own words, §2):

> "What path do I mean? The emulous imitation of the great poets and prose-writers of the past. On this mark, dear friend, let us keep our eyes ever steadfastly fixed. Many gather the divine impulse from another's spirit, just as we are told that the Pythian priestess... conceives from that source the godlike gift of prophecy... so likewise from the mighty genius of the great writers of antiquity there is carried into the souls of their rivals... an effluence which breathes upon them until, even though their natural temper be but cold, they share the sublime enthusiasm of others."

The oracular analogy is doing real work here: the model author's genius is figured as a possessing spirit, not a text to be copied. Longinus is explicit that this is not imitation in the sense of borrowing content — he distinguishes it from plagiarism a few lines later (§4): the process instead "resembles the process of copying from fair forms or statues or works of skilled labour." Plato's philosophical prose is said to have bloomed with poetic imagery precisely because he "engaged heart and soul in a contest for precedence with Homer, like a young champion entering the lists against a veteran."

## Chapter XIV — The imagined judge

Longinus generalizes the emulation principle into a compositional technique: the writer should imagine how Homer, Plato, Demosthenes, or Thucydides would have handled the same material, using them "like beacons to guide us." He extends this into an imagined future audience as well — asking how "future ages through all time" will read the work, which he says disciplines the writer against producing anything "crude, maimed, and abortive."

## Why this matters for the comparative sketch

The emulation passage is the clearest ancient articulation of representation as **self-constituting** rather than merely descriptive or reproductive — closer to Butler's citationality than Plato's or Aristotle's accounts. The emulating writer does not represent a pre-existing self; the self capable of sublimity is *produced* through the act of iterated engagement with the model. This is the strongest classical precedent for treating a critical/interpretive frame (here: the norm of "the sublime," embodied in canonical predecessors) as constitutive of the subject who submits to it — directly parallel to the way Zhang Zhupo's commentarial frame constitutes the moral-gendered categories it claims only to explain in *Jin Ping Mei*.

Note also the plagiarism/imitation distinction Longinus draws (§4) — worth comparing to how Zhang Zhupo distinguishes moral commentary from mere paraphrase or synopsis of the novel's events.
