# Longinus, *On the Sublime*

*Covers: ch. 1–12 • ch. 13–end*
*Syllabus weeks: Mar. 8, 10*

## Preliminary note on attribution and context

"Longinus" is a convenience name — the treatise (Greek *Peri Hypsous*) survives in a single damaged manuscript attributed to "Dionysius or Longinus," and its actual author and date are unknown (likely 1st century CE, though this is debated). Worth knowing for comps: you're reading a text whose authorship is genuinely uncertain, which matters less for the argument than for how you frame it — this is a treatise operating somewhat outside the Plato/Aristotle institutional lineage, closer to rhetorical theory than to philosophy proper.

## I. Ch. 1–12 — defining the sublime and its sources

Longinus breaks from both Plato and Aristotle in a fundamental way: he isn't asking what poetry *is* or how it *works structurally* — he's asking what makes certain passages **overwhelming**, capable of producing ekstasis (being carried outside oneself) rather than mere persuasion or pleasure. The sublime isn't primarily about beauty or correctness; it's about a kind of textual force that short-circuits ordinary judgment.

**Five sources of sublimity** (ch. 8): great thoughts, strong emotion, certain figures of speech and thought, noble diction, and dignified word arrangement. Of these, Longinus treats the first two — greatness of thought and genuine passion — as substantially a matter of the writer's *nature* (natural greatness of soul), while the latter three are teachable technique. This split matters: Longinus is explicitly trying to hold together **innate genius and learnable craft**, refusing to fully collapse into either camp.

**Ch. 9 is the famous chapter** — sublimity as "the echo of a great soul." Longinus's key illustrative move here is his reading of Genesis ("Let there be light, and there was light") as an example of sublime simplicity — a strikingly unusual citation for a Greek rhetorician, and worth flagging for comps as an early instance of a pagan critic finding aesthetic power in a non-Greek/Hebrew text, outside his own culture's canon.

## II. Ch. 13–end — imitation, emulation, and the sublime as excess

The later chapters extend the theory in ways that connect directly to the mimesis unit: Longinus recommends **emulation of great predecessors** (Homer, Plato, Demosthenes) not as slavish copying but as a kind of agonistic self-measurement — the writer competes with the greats and, through that competition, is elevated beyond their own natural capacity. This is a third model of mimesis, distinct from both Plato's possession-model (*Ion*) and Aristotle's craft-model (*Poetics*): imitation here is neither passive channeling nor neutral rule-following, but a **contest that transforms the imitator**.

Longinus is also unusually willing to defend **flawed greatness over flawless mediocrity** — a writer who occasionally fails while reaching for something immense is superior to one who never errs because they never risk anything. This is a genuinely distinctive move against the whole classical tradition's emphasis (especially Aristotle's) on correctness, proportion, and the avoidance of error. The sublime, for Longinus, is inherently a matter of risk and excess — it can't be safety-checked into existence.

The treatise closes (in a famously abrupt, possibly incomplete ending) with a historical/political argument: sublimity is in decline in his own era, and he entertains — without fully endorsing — the idea that a loss of political freedom under empire saps the greatness of soul that produces sublime writing. This links rhetorical/aesthetic power directly to political conditions, a move neither Plato nor Aristotle makes in the assigned material.

## Comparative/throughline notes for comps

- **Against the craft/possession binary set up by *Ion* and the *Poetics*:** Longinus's genius/technique split is a genuine third position, and it's the one later reception history (especially 18th-century "genius" aesthetics, and eventually Romanticism) runs with far more than either Plato's possession or Aristotle's rule-governed craft. Worth tracking this as the seed of an aesthetic vocabulary — "genius," "greatness of soul," productive error — that becomes dominant much later and that neither Plato nor Aristotle has quite the conceptual apparatus for.
- **The "convincing impossibility over unconvincing possibility" logic from *Poetics* 24–5** and Longinus's "flawed greatness over flawless mediocrity" are structurally similar risk-tolerant aesthetics, worth pairing directly in an exam answer if a question asks you to trace attitudes toward error/impossibility across the tradition.
- **For your own research:** Longinus's emulation-as-contest model of imitation is a useful third term to set against the *Jin Ping Mei* commentarial material. Zhang Zhupo's relationship to the text he's editing isn't quite Plato's possession, isn't quite Aristotle's neutral craft — it's closer to Longinus's agonistic emulation, a commentator competing with and trying to be transformed/elevated by the text he's making legible, while simultaneously disciplining it. Might be worth asking whether commentary-as-genre in the Chinese tradition has an indigenous equivalent to Longinus's "great soul answering a great soul" dynamic, or whether that's a category error to import.
- **Political framing at the end (freedom and the decline of sublimity)** is a rare moment in this reading list where an ancient critic explicitly ties aesthetic power to political structure rather than treating poetics as a self-contained formal or psychological question — worth flagging against Plato's *Republic*, which links poetry and politics constantly but in the opposite direction (poetry as a threat requiring political control, not an effect of political freedom).

## Questions to bring to seminar / hold for comps

1. Is Longinus's genius/technique split actually stable, or does his insistence on emulation (a learnable, disciplined practice) quietly re-absorb "natural greatness" back into craft?
2. Does "flawed greatness over flawless mediocrity" survive as a coherent aesthetic principle, or is it simply a description of what later readers happen to have canonized?
3. How seriously should the political-decline argument at the end be taken, given the text's damaged and possibly incomplete state — is Longinus making a real causal claim, or performing a rhetorical gesture whose full argument we've lost?
