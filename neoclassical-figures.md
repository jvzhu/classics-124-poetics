# Neoclassical reception — decorum and the rule-bound frame

**Relevant figures:** Horace (*Ars Poetica*, the hinge text between antiquity and neoclassicism), Boileau (*L'Art poétique*), Dryden ("An Essay of Dramatic Poesy"), and the broader French/English neoclassical codification of Aristotle into rule (the unities, decorum, *bienséance*).

## The argument

Neoclassicism converts the earlier, more improvisatory defenses (Aristotle's structural account, Longinus's genius exemption) into codified rules:

1. **Horace's *utile et dulce*** ("to teach and to delight," *Ars Poetica* 343) hardens Sidney's later formula into a compact slogan and a set of technical prescriptions (decorum of character, appropriateness of style to subject, the five-act rule).
2. **Decorum (*bienséance*) as the operative category.** French neoclassicism in particular polices not just plot structure but *propriety* — what may be shown, what must happen offstage (violence, in particular, is characteristically reported by a messenger rather than staged). This is an explicit machinery for managing transgressive content through formal displacement rather than removal.
3. **Dryden's comparative method.** "An Essay of Dramatic Poesy" stages a debate between advocates of the ancients, the French, and the English (Shakespearean) traditions — itself a performative demonstration that "correctness" is a contested, tradition-relative standard rather than a discovered universal.

## The external frame

Neoclassicism is the point where the "external frame" becomes fully institutionalized: decorum is no longer an ad hoc defense mounted by an individual critic (Sidney) but a formal rule system that pre-sorts content into permissible and impermissible *before* any given work is written. The frame stops being a response to a text and becomes a condition of the text's production.

## Connection point

This is the closest Western analogue to the *xiuxiang*-edition illustrative and paratextual apparatus (chapter-opening verses, marginal commentary, moralizing chapter titles) that surrounds the *Jin Ping Mei* narrative — a formal system operating alongside the text that pre-shapes how transgressive content is to be received, functioning less like a rule of composition and more like a standing interpretive escort.
