# Plutarch and Augustine: Reading Poetry, Reading Scripture

*Covers: Plutarch,* How Young Men Should Study Poetry *(*De Audiendis Poetis*), §§1–8 (=* Moralia *14d–28d) • Augustine,* Confessions *Book 12*
*Syllabus week: Mar. 29, 31*

## I. Plutarch, *How Young Men Should Study Poetry*

Plutarch (1st–2nd c. CE) is writing a practical pedagogical treatise, not a metaphysical or psychological theory like Plato's or Aristotle's. His premise: young people are going to read poetry regardless of what philosophers say about its dangers — Homer, Hesiod, and the tragedians are simply unavoidable in a Greco-Roman education. So the real question isn't *whether* to allow poetry (Plato's question) but **how to train a reader to extract benefit from material that is often morally compromised, false, or seductive.**

This makes Plutarch, in effect, the syllabus's first sustained answer to the problem Sidney will later face directly: what do you do about the "nurse of abuse" charge if you can't simply ban the offending content?

**Plutarch's toolkit for the young reader** (§§1–8):

- **Attend to authorial judgment embedded in outcomes.** Even when poets depict wicked or foolish characters, they often signal disapproval through the consequences those characters suffer — a competent reader learns to read the *plot's verdict*, not just the surface action, as the poem's real moral content.
- **Use poetry comparatively against itself.** Plutarch recommends setting contradictory statements from different poets, or even the same poet, side by side, so students learn early that poetic authority is not univocal or automatically authoritative — poets disagree with each other and sometimes with themselves.
- **Read poetry as a bridge to philosophy, not a substitute for it.** The young reader should be trained to notice where a poem's phrasing gestures toward a philosophical idea explored more rigorously elsewhere — poetry is scaffolding to be outgrown, not a terminal destination.
- **Correct the poet's bad opinions explicitly, in the act of reading.** Where Plato wants to exclude bad content, Plutarch wants students actively taught to talk back to it — to notice a false or harmful claim (e.g., about the gods, or about happiness) and mentally supply the correction.

**Why this matters for the unit:** Plutarch essentially invents a *reader-training program* to solve the problem Plato raises and Aristotle mostly sidesteps. This is worth holding next to Zhang Zhupo directly — both are responding to the same structural anxiety (readers might absorb the wrong lesson from morally compromised content) with the same structural solution (build an explicit reading method rather than relying on the text's content alone to guarantee virtue). Plutarch is doing, in miniature and centuries earlier, exactly what Zhang Zhupo's commentarial apparatus does at much greater scale and intensity.

## II. Augustine, *Confessions* Book 12 — the hermeneutics of Genesis

Book 12 sits in the last third of the *Confessions* (Books 11–13), where Augustine shifts from autobiography to sustained scriptural exegesis — specifically, extended meditation on the opening verses of Genesis. Book 11 concerns time; Book 12 concerns "the heaven of heaven" and **formless matter** (the "invisible and unformed earth" of Genesis 1:2); Book 13 moves through the days of creation allegorically.

**The passage most relevant to a poetics syllabus** is Augustine's extended argument about **interpretive pluralism**: when he considers the word "beginning" (or other terms in the opening of Genesis), he acknowledges that other readers propose different, sometimes conflicting interpretations of what Moses meant. Rather than insisting his own reading is the single correct one, Augustine argues that **multiple different interpretations can all be legitimately true**, provided each is consistent with the rule of faith and produces charity — because Moses (or God, working through Moses) may have intended a meaning rich enough to generate several true readings simultaneously, even ones the original human author didn't consciously have in mind.

Augustine goes further: he says he would rather have written the text himself so that *every* true interpretation any future reader might discover would already be contained in it, rather than pick just one meaning and exclude the rest. This is an unusually generous, almost proto-reader-response theory of textual meaning for a 4th–5th century thinker — meaning is not simply "what the author intended" in a narrow sense, but something closer to the full range of what a rich, divinely-inspired text can truthfully sustain.

**The constraint that keeps this from being unlimited:** Augustine's pluralism is bounded. An interpretation is admissible only if it doesn't contradict the truth as established by faith and doesn't produce vice — the same move Plutarch makes (readers may need external correction), except Augustine locates the check not in reader training but in **fidelity to a governing rule** (scripture's overall consistency, Church teaching) that arbitrates between admissible and inadmissible readings.

## Comparative/throughline notes for comps

- **Both texts are, at bottom, theories of legitimate multiplicity in reading** — Plutarch trains the reader to hold a poem's surface and its "real" moral content apart; Augustine holds that a single scriptural passage can rightly sustain several true meanings at once. This is worth setting up as a genuine precedent for **why later commentarial traditions feel licensed to produce competing, non-identical readings of the same text without treating this as a scandal.** The Chongzhen commentator and Zhang Zhupo producing opposed readings of the same *Jin Ping Mei* passages isn't a modern hermeneutic anomaly — it has real precedent in exactly this kind of late-antique interpretive theory, even though the traditions are unconnected.
- **The crucial difference is what bounds legitimate multiplicity.** Augustine's boundary is external and doctrinal (consistency with faith, production of charity). Plutarch's boundary is pedagogical and internal to the reading process (the trained reader supplies correction in real time). Zhang Zhupo's boundary is closer to Augustine's — an external, totalizing framework (Confucian ritual propriety, Xunzian moral anthropology) that determines which readings of Jinlian's behavior count as legitimate. The Chongzhen commentator's more sympathetic readings look, by contrast, closer to something without a fixed external rule at all — closer to just attentiveness to represented feeling. Worth using this cluster to sharpen exactly *what kind* of interpretive license each commentarial tradition is claiming, rather than treating "multiple readings exist" as a flat, undifferentiated fact.
- **Augustine's wish that the text could sustain every true reading at once** is a strikingly different attitude toward textual richness than anything in Plato or Aristotle, and worth flagging as a genuine turning point on the syllabus — it's the first text in the sequence where multiplicity of meaning is treated as a *virtue* of a good text rather than a problem to be resolved by finding the single correct reading.

## Questions to bring to seminar / hold for comps

1. Is Plutarch's "read the plot's verdict, not just the action" method actually reliable, or does it just relocate interpretive difficulty onto identifying what counts as narrative punishment/reward in the first place?
2. Does Augustine's interpretive pluralism actually resolve the tension between multiple true readings and a single textual origin, or does it simply defer the problem to whichever authority gets to define "the rule of faith"?
3. If you set Plutarch's reader-training model against Augustine's authority-bounded pluralism, which one better describes what a commentator like Zhang Zhupo is actually doing — training future readers, or policing legitimate meaning?
