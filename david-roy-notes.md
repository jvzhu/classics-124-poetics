# David Tod Roy: *The Plum in the Golden Vase* — Translation and Interpretive Apparatus

*Companion file to `zhang-zhupo-vs-chongzhen.md` and `ding-naifei-notes.md`. Roy is the hinge figure through whom Zhang Zhupo's 17th-century commentarial frame entered — and largely came to dominate — Anglophone scholarship.*

## The project, briefly

David Tod Roy (1933–2016), professor emeritus of Chinese literature at the University of Chicago, produced the first complete, fully annotated English translation of the novel, published by Princeton UP in five volumes across two decades: Volume 1, *The Gathering* (1993), through Volume 5, *The Dissolution* (2013). The title Roy uses throughout — *The Plum in the Golden Vase* — is his own rendering of *Jin Ping Mei*, built from the given names of three central female characters (Pan Jinlian, Li Ping'er, Chunmei), a title choice that itself signals his reading: the novel organized around, and largely defined by, its women.

The translation is based on the 1610 edition, understood as the version closest to the author's original intention, and is fully annotated — extensive endnotes tracing the novel's dense web of borrowed songs, proverbs, and quotations from earlier literature. Reviewers have noted that the annotation apparatus makes the translation genuinely easier to follow than the Chinese original, underscoring how much interpretive scaffolding Roy built around the text rather than simply rendering it.

## Roy's interpretive thesis

Roy's core critical claim, repeated across reviews and the translation's own framing: the novel exhibits an uncompromising moral vision, which he traces to Xunzi's philosophy of human nature as inherently evil, redeemable only through active moral transformation. This is the direct throughline from Zhang Zhupo already covered in this repo — Roy is not proposing a new interpretive frame so much as **exporting Zhang's 1695 Xunzian defense into 20th/21st-century Western sinology**, with his own scholarly authority now backing it.

Consistent with this, Roy reads the novel's explicit sexual content as **purposeful moral critique rather than titillation** — his stated position is that the descriptions of sexual acts function as the most powerful metaphor available to the author for expressing contempt toward people who indulge in such behavior. This is a strong, specific claim: not merely that the novel contains a moral frame somewhere in its structure, but that the explicit content is *itself* the vehicle of moral condemnation, deliberately and consistently.

A capsule version of this reading appears in coverage of the fifth volume, which describes the novel's pornographic content as held within an overarching structure of hubris and nemesis, revenge and restitution — the "firm moral structure" formulation associated with Roy throughout the secondary literature.

Commentary echoing Roy's reading has framed the novel's larger argument as a diagnosis of a breakdown in traditional relationships — the household at the story's center is undone because its most basic bonds (husband and wife, parent and child) have come apart from the ritual order that was meant to hold them together. That framing explicitly presents itself as continuing a traditional line of interpretation, i.e., self-consciously following the Zhang Zhupo reading rather than breaking from it.

## The pushback — ambivalence vs. verdict

The clearest scholarly counterweight in the material gathered here comes from a review of Volume 4 in *Translation and Literature*, which pushes directly on Roy's insistence on a clean moral verdict. The review notes Roy's language describing the protagonist's "failure to assume moral responsibility for one's own actions," and contrasts this with what the reviewer sees in the text itself: a dark exuberance that colors not just the protagonist's misdeeds but an entire surrounding culture of venality and petty corruption, narrated with a reticence the reviewer calls "militant" and pointedly un-Dickensian — meaning the implied author declines to supply the kind of moralizing commentary a Victorian social novelist would layer over comparable material. The implication: Roy's Xunzian "firm moral structure" may be a real strand in the text, but treating it as *the* structure risks flattening a narrative voice that is closer to withholding judgment than delivering it.

This matters directly for the Ding Naifei file: Ding's critique of the misogynistic reading-tradition and this reviewer's independent complaint about over-firm moralizing are not the same argument, but they converge on the same diagnosis — **Roy's insistence on a clear, Xunzian moral verdict may be doing more interpretive work than the text itself licenses**, work inherited wholesale from Zhang Zhupo's commentarial frame rather than argued fresh.

## Why Roy matters structurally, not just as one more scholar

Two things make Roy specifically important for a comps argument, beyond just being "a scholar who agrees with Zhang Zhupo":

1. **Roy is the primary conduit** through which most Anglophone readers, including scholars outside Chinese literature specifically, encounter the novel at all — his is the standard complete translation. This means Zhang Zhupo's 17th-century interpretive frame doesn't just survive in specialist Chinese-literature scholarship; it gets built into the paratextual apparatus (title choice, annotation, introductions) of the text most non-specialists will ever read. Ding Naifei's complicity charge against "modern aesthetic/formalist scholarship" lands with particular force here because Roy's edition is not a niche academic intervention — it's the *default* English-language *Jin Ping Mei*.

2. **Roy's title itself is an interpretive act.** *The Plum in the Golden Vase*, built from three women's names, foregrounds the novel's female characters in exactly the way Ding argues the underlying commentarial tradition reduces those same characters to abstracted moral types (the *yinfu*, etc.). There's a real tension worth pressing in a comps answer: does Roy's title choice work against his Xunzian-diagnostic reading (centering the women as the narrative's actual subject) or reinforce it (packaging three women's names as the emblem of a household's ruin, in service of the "hubris and nemesis" structure)?

## Working thesis for this file

Roy functions as the **transmission point** in the three-way structure this repo has been building (Sidney / Zhang Zhupo / Ding Naifei's critique): he doesn't just report Zhang's reading, he re-authorizes it with the weight of a monumental, decades-long, fully annotated scholarly translation — making Zhang's 1695 Xunzian defense the working assumption of most subsequent English-language engagement with the novel, scholarly and general-reader alike, exactly as Ding's genealogy argues.

## Open questions

1. Is it possible to use Roy's translation and annotation — genuinely valuable as philological work — while explicitly setting aside his Xunzian interpretive frame, or does the frame shape the annotation choices themselves too pervasively to separate cleanly?
2. Does the *Translation and Literature* reviewer's "radical ambivalence" reading (implied author withholding judgment) offer a genuine third position beyond both Roy's firm-moral-structure reading and Ding's critique of that reading — or does ambivalence-as-interpretation risk its own kind of evasion (declining to take a position on whether the misogynistic reading tradition is itself a problem)?
3. How much should Roy's title choice (centering three women's names) count as evidence for or against the charge that his edition perpetuates a reductive reading of those same women?
