# Zhang Zhupo (1695) vs. the Chongzhen commentator

## Two commentarial layers, two recensions

- **Chongzhen commentator** — anonymous, associated with the *xiuxiang* (繡像本, illustrated) recension, roughly contemporary with the Chongzhen reign (1628–1644). Marginal and interlinear comments, generally sparser and more moralistic-in-passing than systematic.
- **Zhang Zhupo (張竹坡)** — writing in 1695, produces a full critical apparatus for a *xiuxiang*-descended edition: a lengthy set of prefatory essays (讀法, "how to read"), chapter-by-chapter commentary, and a systematic account of the novel's structural logic.

## Zhang Zhupo's core claims

1. **The novel is a *warning* (懲戒), not an incitement.** Ximen Qing's sexual and financial excesses are structured to produce their own retribution; the text's apparent indulgence in transgressive content is, on Zhang's reading, always already organized by a moral arc that punishes it.
2. **Structural parallelism as evidence of authorial control.** Zhang's *dufa* essays catalog elaborate parallels and echoes across chapters (paired characters, mirrored scenes, numerological patterning) as proof that a single, morally serious author is in total command of the material — the opposite of a text that has simply run away with its own prurience.
3. **The "cold" and "hot" (冷熱) rhetoric.** Zhang repeatedly frames the novel's emotional temperature — moments of excess "heat" (indulgence) are always paired with "cold" (consequence, decline, death) — as a deliberate thermodynamic structure, not incidental content.

## Contrast with the Chongzhen commentator

The Chongzhen-layer comments are comparatively local and reactive — moral asides at particular scenes — rather than a totalizing structural theory. Zhang Zhupo's innovation is to convert scattered moral disapproval into a systematic argument that the entire novel's architecture *is* a moral machine. This is a stronger and more theoretically ambitious version of the "external frame" move: Zhang is not just telling the reader how to feel about a scene, he is building an interpretive system that pre-determines how any scene must be read.

## Open question for the comparative project

Is Zhang Zhupo's structural theory better read as Aristotelian (plot-as-moral-architecture) or Longinian (Zhang's own critical genius as the guarantor of the reading)? Zhang's prefatory rhetoric leans on both: claims about the text's objective structure (Aristotelian) and claims about his own privileged insight as reader/critic (Longinian, or closer to Sidney's "right reader"). This dual appeal is itself worth treating as a distinct pattern, not just an imported Western hybrid.
