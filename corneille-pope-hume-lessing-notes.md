# The Neoclassical Tail: Corneille, Pope, Hume, Lessing

*Covers: Corneille, selections from the three treatises on drama • Pope,* Essay on Criticism *• Hume, "Of the Standard of Taste" • Lessing,* Laocoön *(selections)*
*Syllabus weeks: Apr. 12, 14 — Apr. 19, 21*

## I. Corneille — the three discourses on drama

Corneille's *Trois discours* (1660, prefacing a collected edition of his plays) are a working dramatist's retrospective defense of his own practice against strict neoclassical rule-mongers, written in the wake of the *Cid* controversy (his 1637 play was attacked by the Académie française for violating classical propriety and the unities).

**Core moves worth knowing:**

- Corneille defends **modifying Aristotle's rules rather than abandoning or blindly obeying them** — he treats the *Poetics* as authoritative but historically contingent, arguing Aristotle described Greek practice rather than laying down timeless law, which licenses adaptation to French theatrical conditions.
- On the **unities** (time, place, action): Corneille accepts them in principle but argues for maximum practical flexibility — extending the unity of time beyond a strict 24 hours where necessary, treating unity of place as satisfiable by a single city or palace rather than one room, and defending unity of action as compatible with subplots so long as they causally serve the main action.
- On **pity and fear** (engaging directly with the *Poetics* material you already have notes on): Corneille argues a tragedy doesn't need to produce *both* emotions in full measure from every character — one principal character can inspire fear, another pity, and the play can succeed on the combination rather than requiring both from a single figure's arc, a real departure from the tighter Aristotelian requirement.
- Corneille is explicit that the dramatist's **first obligation is to please an audience**, with adherence to rules subordinate to that goal — rules are means, not ends, and a rule that damages a play's power to move its audience should yield.

**Why this matters for the unit:** Corneille is the syllabus's first working artist (not philosopher or rhetorician) theorizing from inside professional practice, openly negotiating between inherited classical authority and contemporary audience demands. This is a genuinely different genre of poetics text than anything earlier on the list — closer to a craft manual with polemical self-defense built in.

## II. Pope, *Essay on Criticism* (1711)

A verse treatise (heroic couplets) on literary judgment, written when Pope was in his early twenties, synthesizing a broadly neoclassical position indebted to Aristotle, Horace, Longinus, and Boileau.

**Key positions:**

- **"Nature" as the ultimate standard**, but Nature understood as already codified in classical rule — Pope's famous formulation treats the ancient rules not as arbitrary constraints but as Nature methodized: the rules *are* nature, correctly generalized, so following the ancients and following nature are, properly understood, the same act.
- **Wit and judgment must balance** — wit (invention, brilliance) without judgment (discipline, correctness) produces flashy but unsound work; judgment without wit produces correct but lifeless work. Neither alone suffices.
- **License for genius to break rules** — Pope allows that a "grace beyond the reach of art" sometimes justifies a great writer transgressing a rule that would bind a lesser one, provided the transgression achieves something the rule was designed to protect in the first place. This is a real echo of Longinus's flawed-greatness-over-flawless-mediocrity position, imported into a much more rule-bound, neoclassical framework than Longinus's own.
- **Extensive treatment of critical vice** — pride, partiality, envy, fashion-chasing — Pope spends substantial space on the ethics and psychology of *bad criticism* specifically, not just bad poetry, effectively extending Plutarch's reader-training project to the professional critic rather than the young student.

**Why this matters for the unit:** Pope is attempting a grand synthesis of the whole tradition you've been reading — Aristotelian rule, Longinian genius, Horatian decorum — into a single coherent (if conservative) neoclassical position, in verse, for an English audience. He's useful as a barometer of where a century and a half of accumulated poetics theory (Sidney through Corneille) had settled by the early 18th century.

## III. Hume, "Of the Standard of Taste" (1757)

A genuine philosophical departure from everything preceding it on the syllabus — Hume is asking a meta-question none of the earlier texts pose directly: **given that taste is admittedly subjective and variable, is there any standard by which some aesthetic judgments can be called better than others?**

**Core argument:**

- Hume accepts the empiricist premise that beauty is not a property of objects but a **sentiment produced in the observer** — famously, "beauty is no quality in things themselves, it exists merely in the mind which contemplates them." This is a real break from every prior text on the syllabus, all of which (Plato, Aristotle, Plotinus especially) treat beauty as in some sense a real feature of the object or a Form the object participates in.
- Despite this subjectivism, Hume argues a real standard exists, located not in objects but in the **converging verdict of qualified judges** — critics with delicacy of taste, practice/experience, freedom from prejudice, and good sense, whose judgments, when those conditions are met, reliably converge across time and culture (he cites the durability of Homer's reputation as evidence).
- He allows for genuinely irresolvable variation rooted in **age, temperament, and culture** (his famous example: a young reader may reasonably prefer different qualities than an old one) as distinct from *defects* in the observer (prejudice, lack of practice) that a standard can legitimately correct for.

**Why this matters for the unit:** Hume relocates the whole poetics tradition's question from "what is beauty/the good poem" onto "who is qualified to judge, and why do their judgments converge" — a shift toward reception and critical authority that has more in common with your interest in *commentary as a site of meaning-production* than almost anything else on this syllabus. Worth flagging directly: Hume's "qualified judges whose convergent verdicts constitute the standard" is structurally close to what a commentarial tradition (Zhang Zhupo, Chongzhen) is implicitly claiming for itself — authority derived not from the text alone but from the trained, converging judgment of the right kind of reader.

## IV. Lessing, *Laocoön* (1766) — the boundary between poetry and visual art

Lessing's treatise (prompted by debate over the sculpture group of Laocoön and his sons) argues **poetry and painting/sculpture are not the same kind of imitation and shouldn't be judged by the same standards** — a direct challenge to the Horatian *ut pictura poesis* ("as is painting, so is poetry") tradition that had authorized treating the two arts as interchangeable.

**Core argument:**

- Visual art works in **space**, and is therefore suited to representing bodies and their coexisting features at a single instant; poetry works in **time** (sequential language, unfolding word by word), and is therefore suited to representing actions and events unfolding in succession.
- Lessing's central case study: the sculpture shows Laocoön's suffering restrained, dignified, not screaming — while Virgil's account (in the *Aeneid*) has him crying out in agony. Lessing argues this isn't inconsistency between the two artists but a **necessary consequence of medium**: sculpture, fixing one permanent instant for the eye to dwell on indefinitely, must choose a moment of dignified restraint (an unrestrained scream frozen forever would be aesthetically unbearable), while poetry, moving through time, can show and pass through a moment of extremity without the viewer being trapped staring at it.
- This yields a general rule: painting/sculpture should choose the **single most pregnant moment** (*der fruchtbarste Augenblick*) that best implies what came before and after, since it can't show sequence directly; poetry has much greater freedom to depict ugliness, violence, and extremity precisely because it doesn't fix a single permanent image before the eye.

**Why this matters for the unit:** Lessing is the first text on the syllabus to argue rigorously that **medium determines the ethics and aesthetics of representation** — the same content (Laocoön's suffering) is appropriate in one medium and inappropriate in another, not because of differing moral content but because of how each medium's formal properties structure the audience's experience of duration and permanence.

## Comparative/throughline notes for comps

- **This unit as a whole documents the breakdown of a unified "poetics."** Everything earlier on the syllabus (however much internal disagreement) shares an assumption that there's one general theory of poetic representation to be had. By the end of this sequence, that assumption has fractured three ways: Corneille localizes authority in professional practice and audience effect; Hume relocates it in the psychology and sociology of qualified judgment; Lessing insists representation can't even be theorized as one thing across media. A strong comps answer could use this unit to narrate the syllabus's own implicit argument: from a search for the single correct theory of mimesis (Plato/Aristotle) to an acknowledgment of medium-, judge-, and practice-relative standards (Corneille/Hume/Lessing).
- **Lessing's medium-specificity argument is a genuinely useful tool for your own work** on cross-genre or cross-medium representations of the body (fiction vs. theatrical performance vs. illustrated editions of *Jin Ping Mei*, for instance) — his claim that the *same* content demands different treatment depending on whether it's fixed permanently before the eye (a printed illustration, a staged tableau) or unfolding in sequential time (narrated prose) gives you a rigorous, historically grounded reason to expect illustrated/staged versions of a text to handle explicit or violent content differently from the prose narrative itself, independent of any differing moral judgment about the content.
- **Hume's "qualified judges" standard** is worth pairing directly with the Augustine/Plutarch material on interpretive authority — three different historical answers (Augustine's doctrinal rule, Plutarch's trained individual reader, Hume's converging community of qualified critics) to the same underlying question: what actually licenses one reading or judgment over another, once you admit texts can sustain multiple responses?

## Questions to bring to seminar / hold for comps

1. Does Corneille's audience-pleasure standard actually conflict with Aristotelian theory, or is it a permissible extension Aristotle's own text (which repeatedly appeals to what produces the strongest pity/fear effect) already implies?
2. Is Hume's "qualified judges converge" standard actually non-circular — how would you identify a qualified judge except by their judgments already matching the supposed standard?
3. Does Lessing's space/time medium distinction hold up against narrative visual media that unfold over time (a scroll painting, a sequence of illustrated scenes, theater itself) — or is his binary really painting-vs-poetry rather than space-vs-time as such?
